Why the Catholic branch has the older rite: the East Syriac Rite was the original liturgy of the whole undivided church, inherited from its Persian bishops over some fifteen centuries. The West Syriac Rite arrived only after 1665, brought by Syriac Orthodox bishops from Antioch to the branch that had rejected Rome at the Coonan Cross. So the Rome-aligned Syro-Malabar Church preserved the ancient Eastern rite (Latinized, later restored), while today's Orthodox and Jacobite churches worship in the rite adopted later — the reverse of what the labels might suggest.

Why are there two rites at all? Both grew from the same Aramaic-speaking Christianity of Edessa, but an imperial border cut the Syriac world in two. Christians inside the Roman Empire looked to Antioch; Christians across the frontier in the Persian (Sasanian) Empire built their own hierarchy at the capital, Seleucia-Ctesiphon, declaring administrative independence in 424 — because Persian rulers, forever at war with Rome, suspected their Christian subjects of being a fifth column for the enemy (the suspicion behind Shapur II's great 4th-century persecution). Theology then hardened the split: the Persian church did not accept the Council of Ephesus (431) — and when Rome closed the School of Edessa, its scholars migrated to Nisibis in Persia, sealing the eastern church's Antiochene-school theology and its 'Nestorian' reputation, a label it never used for itself. Meanwhile Syria's opponents of Chalcedon (451) were organized by Jacob Baradaeus (d. 578) into the Syriac Orthodox Church of Antioch, nicknamed 'Jacobite' after him. Two empires, two councils, two rites — and India, sitting at the far end of the Persian trade routes, naturally received the East Syriac rite first. The podcast maps the same geography: Latin in the west, Greek in the center, and Syriac 'from Antioch and Edessa going east, all the way to India.' Modern dialogues, it should be said, treat the two ancient Christologies as largely compatible — divided as much by language and imperial politics as by faith.
